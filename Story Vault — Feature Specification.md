Story Vault — Feature Specification
Priority
This is the daily engagement engine. The CI Dashboard captures intelligence. The Story Vault is what makes reps open StreetNotes every single day — including days without calls. It scored as the strongest retention feature in the evaluation because it simultaneously solves the skill development gap, creates a gamified practice loop, generates a viral sharing mechanic ("Beat My Score"), and builds a knowledge-sharing network effect that compounds with every user.

Why No Competitor Has This
All 8 voice-to-CRM competitors (Hey DAN, CallJune, Sybill, aiOla, White Cup, Ledbeam, RocketPhone, RepMove) focus exclusively on capturing what happened on a call. None of them ask the question: how do we make the rep better at the next call? Conversation intelligence platforms like Gong and Chorus analyze recorded calls after the fact, but none make reps practice speaking their stories aloud and score their delivery with specific, actionable coaching. Sales training platforms like SalesHood and Lessonly deliver content but do not score live voice performance. The Story Vault fills that gap — AI-assisted drafting, voice-recorded practice, six-dimension scoring, and Duolingo-style gamification in a single feature.

How It Works
The Story Vault helps reps build, practice, and perfect three types of sales stories. AI assists the drafting process through structured prompting — it draws the story out of the rep, it does not write it for them. A practice module records the rep telling their story aloud, scores their delivery across six weighted dimensions, and provides detailed improvement guidance with specific examples. The rep's highest-scoring version of each story type is displayed in their personal Vault. All approved users within an organization can browse the team's highest-scoring stories.

Three Story Types
Story Type 1: Elevator Pitch
A concise, compelling pitch that answers "What do you do and why should I care?" in 30, 60, or 90 seconds. The AI drafting assistant helps the rep build their pitch by asking structured questions: "What is the one problem your prospect cares about most?" then "What does your solution do about it — in one sentence?" then "What is the proof it works — one specific result or customer example?" then "What is the ask — what do you want them to do next?" The AI assembles the answers into a draft, then refines it with the rep through conversation — tightening language, eliminating filler, sharpening the hook. The rep can generate multiple versions (30s, 60s, 90s) and practice each.

Story Type 2: Feel, Felt, Found — Objection Handling Stories
For every common objection the rep encounters, the AI helps them build a Feel/Felt/Found response. Feel: acknowledge the prospect's concern with genuine empathy, mirror their exact language. Felt: introduce social proof — another customer or prospect who felt the same way. Found: deliver the resolution — what that customer found after moving forward. The AI drafting assistant prompts: "What objection do you hear most often? Type or speak it exactly as prospects say it." Then: "Think of a customer or prospect who had the same concern. What was their situation?" Then: "What happened after they moved forward? What specific result did they get?" The AI assembles the Feel/Felt/Found story, then coaches the rep on making the empathy genuine (not formulaic), the social proof specific (not generic), and the resolution concrete (not vague). Common objections to build stories for: "We already have a solution for that," "We don't have budget right now," "I need to talk to my boss," "Just send me some information," "We're not looking at this right now," "How is this different from [competitor]?"

Story Type 3: Customer Stories Using ABT (And, But, Therefore)
The most powerful sales tool is a story about a customer who looks exactly like the prospect. The AI helps the rep build these stories using the ABT framework. And: establish the context — "We work with a company about your size, AND they were running into the same challenge with [specific pain point]..." But: introduce the conflict — "BUT they were losing [specific metric] because [specific problem], and their team was [specific frustration]..." Therefore: deliver the resolution — "THEREFORE they [took specific action], and within [timeframe] they saw [specific result]." The AI drafting assistant prompts: "Describe the customer — industry, size, role of the person you worked with." Then: "What was the situation they were dealing with? (the AND)" Then: "What was the specific problem or pain that created urgency? (the BUT)" Then: "What did they do, and what was the measurable result? (the THEREFORE)" The AI assembles the ABT story, then coaches on making the AND relatable to the target prospect, making the BUT create genuine tension, and making the THEREFORE specific and credible.

Practice Engine (6 Scoring Dimensions)
This is the core differentiator. No other tool makes reps practice speaking their stories aloud and scores their delivery.

The rep selects a story to practice, clicks "Practice," and records themselves telling the story aloud. The same speech-to-text engine used for voice debriefs transcribes the recording. The AI evaluates the recording across six weighted dimensions and produces an overall score from 0 to 100.

Dimension 1: Framework Adherence (25%)
Did the rep follow the correct structure? For elevator pitches: Problem, Solution, Proof, Ask. For objection handling: Feel, Felt, Found sequence. For customer stories: ABT arc. The system compares the transcript structure against the expected framework template and flags missing or out-of-order elements.

Dimension 2: Clarity (20%)
Was the message clear and concise? Filler words ("um," "uh," "like," "you know") are counted and penalized. Tangents — sentences that do not advance the story — are identified and flagged. The system measures signal-to-noise ratio: how much of the recording delivers the core message versus how much is filler or digression.

Dimension 3: Confidence (20%)
Voice analysis: tone, pace, energy. Did the rep sound certain or hesitant? Upward inflection at the end of statements (making statements sound like questions) is flagged. Vocal energy drops in the middle of the story are identified. The system distinguishes between conversational confidence and aggressive delivery — the goal is authoritative, not loud.

Dimension 4: Pacing (15%)
Was the delivery too fast, too slow, or well-timed? The system measures words per minute across the recording and identifies sections where pacing deviates from the target range (140-160 WPM for conversational delivery). Did key points get appropriate emphasis and pauses? A deliberate pause before a critical number or result signals confidence. Rushing through the proof point signals nervousness.

Dimension 5: Specificity (10%)
Did the story include concrete details — names, numbers, timeframes, results — or was it vague and generic? The system detects concrete nouns, numbers, timeframes, and named entities versus vague language ("great results," "significant improvement," "a lot of companies"). Specificity is what makes a story believable. Every vague phrase is flagged with a suggested replacement.

Dimension 6: Brevity (10%)
Was the story concise enough for the target format? A 60-second elevator pitch that runs 90 seconds is penalized. A Feel/Felt/Found story that takes 3 minutes when it should take 90 seconds is flagged. The system measures total duration against the target and identifies which section ran long.

Detailed Improvement Guidance
After scoring, the AI provides a detailed explanation — not a generic "good job" but specific, actionable coaching with concrete examples:

"Your ABT structure was strong, but the BUT section ran 22 seconds. The tension should be a sharp pivot — aim for 8-10 seconds. Try: 'BUT they were losing 3 deals a month because reps couldn't access account history in the field.'"

"You used 'um' 7 times in 60 seconds. That drops your confidence score from 81 to 64. Try pausing silently instead — a deliberate pause sounds more authoritative than a filler word."

"Your THEREFORE was vague: 'they saw great results.' Replace with a specific number: 'they increased pipeline accuracy from 40% to 92% in 30 days.' Specificity is what makes the story believable."

"Your pacing was rushed in the first 15 seconds — you were speaking at 180 words per minute. Slow the opening to 140 WPM. The hook needs room to land."

"Strong Feel/Felt/Found structure. One improvement: in the FEEL section, mirror the prospect's exact words instead of paraphrasing. If they said 'We're drowning in tools,' say 'Drowning in tools — I hear that constantly' not 'I understand you have a lot of tools.'"

The rep can immediately re-record and see their score change. The system tracks every attempt so the rep can see their improvement over time.

The Vault: Best Stories on Display
Each rep has a personal Vault that automatically displays their highest-scoring version of each story type: best elevator pitch (highest-scoring recording with score breakdown and transcript), best objection handling story (highest-scoring Feel/Felt/Found story with the specific objection it addresses), and best customer story (highest-scoring ABT story with score breakdown and transcript). The Vault updates automatically when the rep beats their previous best score. The previous version is archived (not deleted) so the rep can review their progression.

Team Vault: Approved Access to Top Stories
All approved users within an organization can browse a Team Vault that displays the highest-scoring stories across the entire team: top-scoring elevator pitch across all reps, top-scoring objection handling story for each common objection, and top-scoring customer story across all reps. New reps can listen to the team's best stories as learning examples. This creates a knowledge-sharing flywheel — the best stories become training material for the entire team. Access control: only approved users see the Team Vault. Stories are shared to the Team Vault automatically when they are the highest-scoring version, but a rep can opt out of sharing any specific story if they prefer.

Gamification System (7 Mechanics)
The gamification system is designed to drive daily practice. It borrows proven mechanics from Duolingo — the strongest retention engine in practice-based apps — and applies them to sales skill development.

Mechanic 1: Daily Practice Streaks
Track consecutive days of practice. Display the streak prominently on the dashboard — not buried in a settings page, but visible every time they log in. Send push notifications to maintain streaks: "You're on a 12-day streak — don't break it!" Research from Duolingo confirms that streak mechanics are the single strongest retention driver in practice-based apps. A broken streak should feel like a loss. The system should offer a "streak freeze" (one per week) to prevent frustration from a single missed day.

Mechanic 2: XP Points and Levels
Award XP for every practice session. Bonus XP for improvement (scoring higher than the previous attempt on the same story). Bonus XP for practicing all three story types in one day ("Triple Threat" bonus). 10 levels: Rookie (0 XP), Storyteller (100), Narrative Builder (250), Pitch Pro (500), Story Craftsman (800), Persuasion Specialist (1,200), Objection Slayer (1,700), Master Narrator (2,500), Vault Legend (3,500), Story Vault Champion (5,000). Levels are displayed on the leaderboard alongside other performance metrics.

Mechanic 3: Achievement Badges
Award badges for specific milestones. Badges should be visually distinctive and displayed in the rep's profile. 10 badges: First Story (complete first practice recording), Perfect Pitch (score 95+ on an elevator pitch), Objection Master (score 90+ on 3 different objection handling stories), ABT Architect (score 90+ on a customer story), 7-Day Streak (practice 7 consecutive days), 30-Day Streak (practice 30 consecutive days), Triple Threat (practice all 3 story types in one day), Most Improved (biggest score improvement in a single week), Top of the Vault (have the highest-scoring story on the team for any category), Century Club (complete 100 practice sessions).

Mechanic 4: Daily Challenge
Each day, the system presents a specific challenge: "Today's challenge: Beat your elevator pitch score by 5 points" or "Today's challenge: Practice the 'no budget' objection — your current best is 72." Daily challenges create a reason to open the app every day beyond just maintaining the streak.

Mechanic 5: Leaderboard Integration
Story Vault metrics integrate with the existing leaderboard. Managers see: practice frequency (sessions per week), average scores by story type, improvement trajectory (are scores going up?), current streak length, and XP level. This gives managers a coaching tool — they can identify which reps are investing in their craft and which need encouragement.

Mechanic 6: Weekly Practice Summary
Every Monday, each rep receives a summary of their previous week's practice: sessions completed, scores achieved, improvement vs. prior week, streak status, and XP earned. The summary includes one specific coaching recommendation: "Your elevator pitch scores are strong (avg 84) but your objection handling is lagging (avg 67). This week, focus on the 'already have a solution' objection — it's the one you'll hear most."

Mechanic 7: "Beat My Score" Viral Mechanic
Reps can share their Story Vault scores externally — on LinkedIn, in Slack, via text. The shared card shows the rep's score, story type, and a challenge: "I scored 91 on my elevator pitch. Can you beat it?" Anyone who clicks the link is taken to StreetNotes where they can try the Story Vault for free. This transforms every practice session into a potential acquisition channel. During launch, influencer scores are published publicly to seed the mechanic: "Morgan Ingram scored 91 on his elevator pitch. Josh Braun scored 88 on his objection handling story. Can your reps beat them?"

Rep-Facing Experience
The rep experience must feel rewarding, not extractive. After every practice session, the rep should see immediate feedback:

Their score with a visual breakdown across all six dimensions
A comparison to their previous best: "You improved 7 points since last Tuesday"
Their streak status and XP earned for the session
Their position on the team leaderboard
A specific, actionable coaching recommendation for their next attempt

This feedback loop transforms practice from "something I should do" into "something I want to do." The psychological contract shifts from obligation to competition — against themselves, against their teammates, and against the influencers on the public leaderboard. No competitor in the voice-to-CRM or sales training space has executed this shift.

Manager Dashboard
Managers see an aggregate view of their team's Story Vault engagement: practice frequency per rep, average scores by story type, improvement trajectories, streak lengths, and XP levels. The dashboard answers three questions: Who is investing in their craft? Who needs encouragement? And what are the team's best stories that should be shared with new hires? The manager can also curate the Team Vault — featuring specific stories, adding context, or recommending stories to specific reps who are struggling with a particular objection or story type.
